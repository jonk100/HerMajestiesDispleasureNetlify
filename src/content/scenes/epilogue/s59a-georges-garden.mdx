t-FADE IN:

s-EXT. FRIAR PARK GARDENS - DAY (1973)

a-The gardens of Friar Park are extraordinary - a mix of Victorian Gothic fantasy and careful English gardening. It is a place that clearly cost a fortune and requires constant attention. The landscape feels both artificial and deeply natural, as if two different worlds decided to meet here and found they liked each other.

a-GEORGE HARRISON (30) is working on a section of the garden that has been previously neglected. He is clearing weeds, turning soil. Patient, methodical, not in a hurry. His movements are practiced, economical, the result of years spent learning how things grow.

a-He wears simple gardening clothes, his beard trimmed, his presence peaceful. This is not the rock star George. This is the gardener George, a role he has grown into with the same dedication he once applied to music.

a-He becomes aware of someone watching and looks up.

a-Standing at the garden gate, looking uncertain, is an older man in a brown raincoat. NIGEL FINCH (52). Not in an operational capacity - he's been in the Falklands for two years by now, but this is clearly set slightly earlier, or perhaps he's returned briefly for leave. He looks like what he is: a civil servant who has wandered slightly out of his way.

a-George looks at him without recognition. Just a man at the gate. There is no suspicion, no fear. Just curiosity.

c-GEORGE
d-Can I help you?

a-Nigel is caught off guard. He wasn't expecting to be noticed.

c-NIGEL
d-I — no. I'm sorry. I was just — the gardens are extraordinary. I didn't mean to intrude.

a-He clearly means it. He's not surveilling. He's just here. The honesty in his voice is surprising, as if he's forgotten how to lie to civilians.

a-George looks at him for a moment. Something in the man's manner — the weariness, the genuine appreciation, the lack of agenda — makes him open the gate rather than close the conversation. This is not about security. This is about something else.

c-GEORGE
d-Come in, then. Mind the sitar.

a-Nigel is confused.

c-NIGEL
d-I beg your pardon?

a-George gestures. There is a sitar resting against a garden wall in the sun. It looks perfectly natural there, as if it grows like the flowers and weeds George has been fighting. The instrument is both art object and spiritual tool, both cultural bridge and personal practice.

c-GEORGE
d-I leave it out sometimes. 
p-(beat)
d-To remind me.

c-NIGEL
d-(quietly)
d-Of what?

a-George returns to his weeding, his back to Nigel. The conversation continues without eye contact, as if they've known each other for years.

c-GEORGE
d-(back to his weeding)
d-That things that seem complicated are usually just... sitting in the garden. Waiting.

a-Nigel stands in the garden for a moment, looking at the sitar, the roses, the extraordinary grounds. The wisdom of the statement lands with quiet force. This is the answer to everything he spent five years trying to manipulate, control, and understand. The simplicity is devastating.

a-The sitar in the sun represents everything: the cultural exploration that began in Episode 2, the spiritual journey that followed, the distance George traveled from the band. Here it is simply... an object in a garden, waiting to be played.

a-Then Nigel nods, thanks George, and leaves. George watches him go, slightly puzzled but not concerned. He returns to his weeding. The garden continues its slow, methodical work.

s-EXT. LANE NEAR FRIAR PARK - CONTINUOUS

a-Nigel walks down the lane away from Friar Park. He takes out his old surveillance notebook - worn, faded, the same one from Episode 2. The leather cover is soft from years of being carried in jacket pockets.

a-He opens it to a blank page. The paper is yellowed with age, but still strong enough to hold more words.

a-He writes one word. We don't see what it is. The writing is precise, controlled, the handwriting of someone who has spent years recording observations about other people. The word could be "waiting," or "garden," or "enough." Whatever it is, it is small.

a-He puts the notebook away and keeps walking. The burden of five years of surveillance seems lighter, somehow. The lane continues toward the main road. The afternoon light is beginning to fade.

a-George continues weeding his garden. The sitar waits in the sun. The complications of five years of MI5 surveillance, of band politics, of cultural misunderstanding, have all been reduced to this: things growing, things waiting, things simpler than anyone imagined.

a-This is not an ending. This is just... what is.

t-FADE OUT.
