t-FADE IN:

s-EXT. FILM SET - DAY (MID-1970s)

a-A film set somewhere warm and sunny - Spain, perhaps, or the south of France. The location feels deliberately artificial, a constructed paradise for a constructed story. Canvas tents, equipment trucks, the constant low hum of filmmaking.

a-RINGO STARR (34) is sitting in a canvas director's chair between takes. He is wearing a ridiculously elaborate costume - something that suggests a historical adventure film, perhaps a pirate or a court jester. The costume is flamboyant, colorful, completely at odds with his natural reserve.

a-He is eating a sandwich and reading a newspaper. The sandwich is simple - ham and cheese, nothing fancy. The newspaper is several days old, passed around the crew until it reached him.

a-He turns to the business pages. There, in a small item that would have been front-page news a decade ago, is the announcement: the Beatles' partnership has been officially dissolved. The legal end of what began as four boys in Liverpool.

a-Ringo reads the item. His expression doesn't change. There is no sadness, no nostalgia, no anger. He reads it with the same attention he gives the sandwich - simple acknowledgment of information that affects his life but doesn't define it.

a-He folds the newspaper. He finishes his sandwich. He takes a sip of tea from a thermos. The movements are economical, practiced, the actions of a man who is comfortable with routine.

a-A PRODUCTION ASSISTANT (20s) approaches, clipboard in hand.

c-PRODUCTION ASSISTANT
d-Mr. Starkey? They're ready for you. Scene 47. The big sword fight.

c-RINGO
p-(wiping his mouth)
d-Right then.

a-He stands up. He adjusts his ridiculous costume, straightening a feather that has drooped. He checks his reflection in a small mirror the assistant holds up. He looks at himself - the flamboyant costume, the simple man underneath. A small smile touches his lips.

a-As he walks toward the set, he passes a craft services table. He takes a biscuit - a digestive, plain and unadorned. He eats it while walking, the same simple pleasure as the sandwich.

a-He marks his spot on the set. The director calls action.

c-DIRECTOR (O.S.)
d-Action!

a-Ringo delivers his line. It's something completely absurd, whatever the film requires - perhaps a declaration of pirate loyalty or a jester's riddle. He delivers it perfectly, with the same straightforward sincerity he brings to everything.

c-DIRECTOR (O.S.)
d-Cut! Print!

a-Everyone on set laughs, including Ringo. The laugh is genuine, unguarded, the same laugh Percy saw in the MI5 file. He is not performing. He is simply enjoying the absurdity of his job.

a-He walks back to his canvas chair. He picks up the newspaper again. He looks at the Beatles item one more time. Then he turns to the sports pages.

a-He reads about cricket scores, about football results, about the simple competitions that continue regardless of what happens to bands or governments or conspiracies.

a-The assistant approaches again.

c-PRODUCTION ASSISTANT
d-Five minutes until we reset, Mr. Starkey.

c-RINGO
p-(not looking up from the sports pages)
d-Right. Time for another cuppa.

a-He pours another tea. The sun shines on the artificial set. The film crew continues their work. The Beatles are officially dissolved. Ringo Starr drinks his tea and reads about cricket.

a-This is not immunity. This is just... life.

t-FADE OUT.
