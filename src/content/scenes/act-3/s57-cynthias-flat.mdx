s-EXT./INT. CYNTHIA'S NEW FLAT - DAY (LATE 1969)

s-A small, quiet flat in a modest London neighborhood. Not Kenwood. This is hers, chosen and paid for with divorce settlement - the first space that has ever belonged only to her. The building is Victorian, converted into flats, the thin walls meaning she can hear her neighbors' lives through the floor.

a-INT. CYNTHIA'S FLAT - CONTINUOUS

a-CYNTHIA (29) sits at a small table by the window. The room is half-unpacked - boxes stacked against walls, some still sealed. She's been here a few months but hasn't quite committed to being here. The afternoon light comes through unfamiliar window, casting long shadows that don't belong to anyone.

a-On the table is a newspaper - folded to the arts pages. She is reading something when something catches her eye. The movement is subtle, a slight stillness, as if time has stopped for just a moment.

a-CLOSE ON THE NEWSPAPER: A small item, not a headline. A footnote. John and Yoko's latest conceptual art project - a bag event, perhaps, or the Bed-In coverage. The photograph in the paper shows John and Yoko in bed, white sheets, at peace. John is laughing. The laugh is genuine, unguarded. He looks happy.

a-Cynthia looks at the photograph for a long moment. Her expression is not angry, not sad. It is something more complicated - the face of a person who has been waiting to feel something and has discovered they feel less than expected. There is no bitterness. No tears. Just a quiet, hollow space where something should be.

a-Her fingers trace the edge of the frame, a gesture that contains years of unspoken maternal love. She remembers Julian at that age - the same questions about John's late nights, the same demands for attention, the same small hands reaching for her. The photograph represents not just her son, but everything she tried to preserve.

a-She folds the newspaper carefully, the precise movements of someone who has spent years handling paper with delicate hands. She sets it on the table, photograph of John's laugh facing away from her. The laugh in the photograph is genuine, unguarded - the laugh of a man who has found peace she could never provide. This is not what breaks her. What breaks her is the quiet recognition that his happiness has nothing to do with her.

a-She looks around her flat - the boxes, the half-unpacked life, the afternoon light through an unfamiliar window. This is not the home she planned. This is not the life she wanted. But it is the life she has. Each box represents a decision she made - to keep something, to leave something, to postpone starting over. The unpacking is not procrastination. It is evidence that she is still becoming someone new, even if slowly.

a-On another table is a photograph of JULIAN (6), school picture, smiling. He wears a blazer, has the same eyes as John. She looks at it for a moment. Her fingers trace the edge of the frame, a gesture that contains years of unspoken maternal love. The photograph represents the future - the reason she is writing this letter.

a-Then she picks up a pen and a piece of paper and begins to write. We don't see what she writes. She writes steadily, without crossing anything out. The pen moves across the page with the confidence of someone who knows exactly what needs to be said. The writing is not hurried. It is deliberate. Each word formed with the precision of a woman who has learned that clarity matters more than anything else.

a-The camera shows glimpses of the words: "My dearest Julian," "I hope you are," "remember when." Simple, direct phrases from a mother who has learned that complexity only confuses the truth. But there are other words too, fragments of sentences she starts and stops: "Your father" (crossed out) "the garden" (crossed out) "different now." She is trying to be honest without being cruel, to acknowledge the changes without dwelling on them.

a-When she finishes, she puts the pen down and reads what she's written. Her eyes scan the page, checking for mistakes, finding none. She makes one small addition, a postscript: "All my love, Mummy." The addition is not an afterthought. It is the most important thing she will say today.

a-Then she folds the paper and puts it in an envelope. The movements are precise, economical, practiced. This is not the first letter she has written to her son since the divorce. It will not be the last.

a-She addresses it. Her handwriting is careful, deliberate, the same hand that wrote thank-you notes after Beatles concerts, the same hand that signed permission slips for school trips. Each letter formed with the precision that suggests this matters more than anything else she will do today. The address is written not just to deliver mail, but to maintain a connection across the gap that institutions created.

a-CLOSE ON THE ENVELOPE: 'Julian' written in her neat script. The address below. A small, ordinary envelope that contains something extraordinary: a mother's attempt to bridge a gap that institutions created, to maintain the human connection that bureaucracy tried to sever.

a-She puts a stamp on it. The small, circular stamp feels both inadequate and exactly right. She looks at it for a moment, turning it over in her fingers. The stamp is not just postage. It is permission. It is official recognition that this letter will travel through systems she cannot control.

a-Then she puts her coat on - a practical wool coat that has seen better days, picks up the envelope, and walks out of the flat. The door closes behind her, not with finality but with the quiet understanding that this chapter is ending.

s-EXT. LONDON STREET - CONTINUOUS

a-She walks to the post box on the corner. A red post box on a grey London street. The street is quiet, residential. People pass by, lost in their own lives. None of them know what this small, ordinary act contains. She walks with the steady pace of someone who knows where she is going and why it matters.

a-She drops the letter in.

a-The sound is metallic, final. The letter disappears into the bureaucratic system that will deliver it to her son. The act is both public and private - a mother's love sent through the same institution that tried to keep them apart.

a-She stands at the post box for a moment, her hand still extended where the letter went. Then she withdraws her hand, puts it in her coat pocket, and walks back toward her flat. Not quickly. Not slowly. Just walking. The pace of someone who has nowhere urgent to be, nothing to rush toward, because the important thing has already been done.

a-The camera stays on the post box. A red post box on a grey London street. The letter is inside, carrying her private act into the world without comment. The post box is both barrier and gateway. It is the same red that Brenda used in Episode 5, the same institutional color that marks both official acts and personal ones. But Cynthia's use of it feels different - not an act of defiance like Brenda's, but one of connection.

a-After a moment, the street continues. People pass. The day goes on.
