t-FADE IN:

s-INT. FALKLANDS OFFICE - DAY (MAY 1970)

a-A small, utilitarian office that feels more like a storage room than a workplace. The walls are thin metal, painted institutional green. The window is thick, designed to withstand wind that never stops. The air smells of salt, damp paper, and the faint metallic tang of isolation.

a-A CIVILIAN ADMINISTRATOR (40s) leads NIGEL FINCH (52) and CLIVE BARROW (48) into the office. The administrator is cheerful, efficient, entirely unaware that he's showing two men the tomb where their careers will die.

c-ADMINISTRATOR
d-(cheerfully, over the wind)
d-Here we are then! Not much, but it's yours. Desks are identical - we believe in equality out here. Coffee's in the corner - instant, I'm afraid. The good stuff doesn't travel well.

a-He gestures to two metal desks that are indeed identical. Each has the same stack of shipping manifests, the same government-issue lamp, the same uncomfortable chair. The desks face each other, as if designed for men who have nothing left to say to each other.

c-ADMINISTRATOR
d-Filing system's straightforward. Anything marked 'urgent' goes in the red tray. Anything marked 'when you get to it' goes in the blue tray. Most everything ends up in the blue tray.

a-Nigel and Clive stand in the doorway. Neither man speaks. They are processing the space - not just an office, but the physical manifestation of their punishment.

c-ADMINISTRATOR
d-(noticing their silence)
d-I know it's not London. But the wind's always like this. You get used to it. Some days you can't hear yourself think. Other days you wish you couldn't.

a-He smiles, as if this is a joke that lands differently in the Falklands. He shows them where the coffee is kept. He explains the heating system. He tells them about the weekly supply plane. He is being helpful. He is being kind. His kindness is more devastating than any punishment.

c-ADMINISTRATOR
d-Well, I'll leave you to it. Settle in. The manifests won't file themselves.

a-He exits. The door closes. The wind howls outside, a constant reminder that they are at the edge of the world.

a-Nigel and Clive walk to their identical desks. They sit down. The chairs make identical scraping sounds against the floor.

a-They look at their identical stacks of shipping manifests. They look at each other. The silence is heavier than the wind.

a-Clive reaches for the radio on his desk. It's a small transistor, the kind that belongs in a kitchen, not a government office. He turns it on. Static, then a news bulletin.

a-RADIO ANNOUNCER (V.O.)
d-...and in business news, the Beatles' partnership has been officially dissolved. The band that defined a generation has formally ended their business relationship, though sources close to the group suggest the musical separation has been in effect for some time...

a-Clive turns the radio off. The silence returns, but it's different now. It's the silence of men who have received the institutional verdict on their work.

a-Clive looks at his shipping manifests. He looks at the wind howling outside the window.

c-CLIVE
p-(softly, to no one)
d-It should have been a masterpiece.

a-Nigel picks up a pen. He opens the first shipping manifest. He begins to read. The pen scratching against paper is the only sound in the room besides the wind.

a-Outside, the Falklands wind continues its endless assault. Inside, two men begin their sentence.

t-FADE OUT.
