t-FADE IN:

s-INT. MI5 REGISTRY - DAY (MARCH 1970)

a-The registry. Late afternoon. The light through the tall, grimy windows is grey and institutional, filtered through decades of London pollution. The room is arranged with Anderson-style symmetry - filing cabinets in perfect rows, desks at exact angles, as if geometry itself could prevent chaos.

a-SIR DEREK FINCHLEY (59) is alone. He works with the quiet efficiency of a man who has buried secrets before. Each movement is precise, economical, devoid of wasted motion. He is performing the final administrative task of a failed operation: archival.

a-He is removing files from an active drawer and placing them in a sealed box. The box is sturdy, government-issue, designed for long-term storage in the damp basements of forgotten government buildings.

a-He works systematically, without expression. His face is a mask of professional neutrality. Each file is handled with the same detached respect, whether it contains five years of paranoia or three sheets of nothing.

a-He removes the Beatles files - all four. LENNON, thick with five years of escalating surveillance and failed psychological manipulation. MCCARTNEY, substantial with establishment grooming and diplomatic pressure. HARRISON, respectable with spiritual withdrawal and cultural exploration. STARR, almost empty with institutional indifference.

a-He removes the Operation Minuet file. The folder that started it all, back in 1965. The MBE ceremony, the initial assessment, the authorization from Prince Philip. The paper feels old, brittle with the weight of history.

a-He removes the Operation Elegy file. The escalation. The more desperate measures. The assassination planning. The file feels heavier than the others, as if containing not just paper but the accumulated gravity of terrible decisions.

a-He removes one more file: a single slim folder. CLIVE BARROW BLUE MEANIE ANIMATION. He opens it briefly. Inside is one progress report, typed on official form:

a-CLOSE ON THE PAGE: '47 cels completed out of approximately 3,000. Methodology: sound. Timeline: 1987. Status: ongoing.'

a-He closes the animation file and places it in the box with the others. The absurdity of it - thousands of animation cels, a timeline extending to 1987, all for a project that will never be completed - is treated with the same bureaucratic seriousness as the assassination plots.

a-He places on top of the box a single unsigned memo. The typing is perfect, each letter formed with the precision of a man who believes language can conceal anything. The memo reads:

a-CLOSE ON THE MEMO: 'This file has been reviewed and assessed as containing no material of ongoing operational significance. Contents classified. Access restricted. No further action required.'

a-The phrase 'No further action required' echoes the 'No action required' from Episode 2's blackboard. The institutional language has come full circle. The same words that once enabled the conspiracy now serve to bury it.

a-He seals the box with official government tape. The sound is final, absolute. The tape makes a perfect rectangle, as if applied by a machine that cannot make mistakes.

a-He looks at it for a moment. The box contains years of effort, millions of pounds, conspiracy, paranoia, failed assassination attempts, and one absurd animation project. It looks identical to a dozen other boxes on the shelf.

a-Then he writes on the outside with a black marker, in his neat civil servant hand: 'Operation Minuet / Operation Elegy. Concluded. Filed.'

a-He places it on a shelf. The shelf already contains a dozen identical boxes. His contribution to the archive is indistinguishable from any other. This is not erasure. This is organization.

a-He turns off the light. The room falls into shadow. The filing cabinets stand like tombstones in the darkness.

a-He exits. The door closes with a quiet click.

a-In the darkness, the box sits on the shelf. Waiting. Nobody will ever find it. Nobody will ever look.

t-FADE OUT.
