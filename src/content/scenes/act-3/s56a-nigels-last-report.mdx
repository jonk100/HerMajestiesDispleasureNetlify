t-FADE IN:

s-INT. MI5 DEBRIEFING ROOM - DAY (MARCH 1970)

a-A sterile, windowless room designed for institutional blame-shifting. The air is thick with the weight of unspoken consequences.

a-NIGEL FINCH (52) and CLIVE BARROW (48) sit opposite each other at a grey metal table. Between them: a stack of forms, a carafe of cold coffee, and the silence of men who have failed.

a-Neither man speaks. They have been here for two hours, processing the aftermath. The forms are completed. The statements are signed. There is nothing left to say.

a-Nigel stares at the stack of completed paperwork. His face is a mask of professional exhaustion. The lines around his eyes are deeper, the set of his mouth is softer than usual. He looks like a man who has finally understood the cost.

a-Clive stares at the carafe. His hands are still. He occasionally makes coffee, more from habit than thirst. The ritual provides structure in a world that has collapsed.

a-This is not a debriefing. This is an administrative burial.

a-Nigel picks up a form. It is the final one: REQUEST FOR REASSIGNMENT TO REMOTE POSTING. The bureaucratic language is perfect, each phrase designed to remove humanity from what comes next.

c-NIGEL
p-(quietly, to no one in particular)
d-I need to process one final form.

a-He begins writing. The pen moves across the page with the same practiced precision he used to document surveillance of others. But this time the subject is himself.

a-CLOSE ON THE FORM: 'Operational Assessment: Subject Finch, Nigel. Demonstrated exceptional competence in managing high-stakes operations requiring diplomatic sensitivity and cultural awareness. However, recent mission outcomes suggest emotional fatigue may be impacting judgment. Recommended: Extended administrative leave. Effective immediately.'

a-He signs the form. The signature is the same neat, professional hand that has signed hundreds of documents authorizing surveillance of others. Now it authorizes his own removal.

a-Clive watches him. There is no envy, no anger. Just the quiet understanding of someone who has seen the machine from both sides.

c-CLIVE
p-(softly)
d-You'll like the Falklands.

a-Nigel looks up. The faintest hint of a smile touches his lips. It is not amusement. It is recognition of the absurdity.

c-NIGEL
p-(nodding slightly)
d-It's... quiet there.

a-He slides the form across the table to Clive. The movement is final.

c-CLIVE
d-(taking the form)
d-Plenty of penguins to count.

a-Nigel stands. The debriefing is over. The administrative burial is complete. He looks around the room one last time - the filing cabinets, the sealed windows, the door that leads not to freedom but to a different kind of institutional assignment.

a-He walks to the door. He does not look back.

t-FADE OUT.
