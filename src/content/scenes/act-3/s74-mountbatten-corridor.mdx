t-FADE IN:

s-INT. BUCKINGHAM PALACE - CORRIDOR - IMMEDIATELY AFTER SCENE 73

a-A long, formal corridor that has witnessed centuries of military men walking toward or away from history. The portraits on the walls are of ancestors who understood duty, honor, and the terrible cost of both.

a-LORD MOUNTBATTEN (69) walks alone. He moves with the practiced grace of a man who has spent his life moving through spaces that demand respect. He carries a brandy glass, still barely touched from Philip's study.

a-He stops at a tall window that looks out onto the Palace grounds. The gardens are perfect, maintained with military precision. Each tree stands at attention. Each flower bed follows strict geometric patterns. The landscape is controlled, ordered, predictable.

a-Everything except the man who gave the order that started this disaster.

a-Mountbatten looks out at the grounds for a long moment. He is not thinking about Philip's rage, not about the Queen's disappointment, not about the institutional failure. He is thinking about the chain of command - the thing that has governed his entire life and career, the thing that requires him to follow a disastrous order from a man he knows is wrong.

a-He takes a sip of brandy. The first sip he's actually taken. The liquid burns slightly, a small physical sensation in a world that has become entirely abstract.

a-Then he takes out a small notebook from his jacket pocket. It is military-issue, bound in dark leather, the kind of notebook that has recorded strategic observations from battles and boardrooms across decades of service. It is not a surveillance notebook. It is a strategist's notebook.

a-He opens it to a fresh page. He takes a pen from his pocket. He writes one line, the handwriting precise, economical, the script of a man who values clarity above all else.

a-CLOSE ON THE PAGE: 'Minuet concluded. Elegy approved. God help us.'

a-The words are not emotional. They are strategic assessment. 'Minuet concluded' - the initial operation failed. 'Elegy approved' - the escalation proceeded. 'God help us' - the institutional verdict from inside the institution. Not ironic. Just accurate.

a-He closes the notebook. He finishes the brandy in one smooth motion. The glass is empty now, as if the act of recording the truth required the liquid courage.

a-He walks on down the corridor. His posture is unchanged, his stride unchanged. He is still Lord Mountbatten, still the Queen's cousin, still the man who will follow orders until his death in 1979 at the hands of another kind of political violence.

a-But as he walks, there is something different in his bearing. The weight of the strategic assessment he has just recorded, the knowledge that he is following orders he knows are wrong, settles into him like a uniform he can never remove.

a-The corridor continues. The portraits watch him pass. History continues its indifferent march.

t-FADE OUT.
