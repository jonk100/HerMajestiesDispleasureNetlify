s-EXT. CHELSEA GALLERY – DAY (1966)

a-A trendy Chelsea gallery opening. Art types mingle with wine glasses. The air smells of pretension and cheap white wine.

a-CLIVE BARROW (43) stands near the entrance, now fully in his "KLAUS" persona:

a-Black turtleneck (still itchy, but he's pretending it doesn't bother him)
a-Corduroy jacket with elbow patches
a-Thick-rimmed glasses he cleans obsessively
a-A beret at what he considers a "thoughtful angle"
a-A canvas messenger bag filled with art books and fake sketches

a-He's attempting to loiter with what he believes is continental sophistication. To everyone else, he looks like a man with severe back problems and an unfortunate fashion sense.

s-INT. MI5 SURVEILLANCE VAN – SAME TIME

a-NIGEL FINCH (50) sits in the driver's seat, eating a cold cheese sandwich. He watches Clive through binoculars, expression deadpan.

c-NIGEL
p-(to himself, with the resignation)
d-Oh, heavens. He's doing the loiter.

a-He takes a bite of sandwich. The bread is dry. He chews slowly, as if each mouthful brings him closer to the sweet release of death.

s-EXT. CHELSEA GALLERY – CONTINUOUS

a-Clive spots his target: a group of art critics discussing something animatedly near a sculpture. He approaches, attempting what he believes is a cool, artistic stride.

c-CLIVE
p-(in a terrible, inconsistent German accent)
d-Ahem. Excuse me. I am... Klaus.

a-The critics turn to look at him. One of them, a sophisticated woman in her 30s, raises an eyebrow.

c-ART CRITIC 1
d-Klaus? Klaus who?

c-CLIVE
p-(puffing his chest out slightly)
d-Klaus... Schmidt. Art critic. I have been published in... many journals.

a-He gestures vaguely with his hands, nearly knocking over someone's wine glass.

c-ART CRITIC 2
p-(skeptical)
d-I'm in the art world. I don't believe I've heard of a Klaus Schmidt.

c-CLIVE
p-(nodding sagely)
d-Ah! That is because I write for... German publications. Very influential. In Berlin. And Munich. And... other German places.

a-He reaches into his messenger bag, pulls out his sketchbook. He flips to a page showing a crudely drawn abstract composition.

c-CLIVE
d-I am here studying the... how you say... cultural penetration of popular music into the avant-garde space. The Beatles, for example.

c-ART CRITIC 1
d-The Beatles? They're not avant-garde. They're pop.

c-CLIVE
p-(nodding sagely)
d-Exactly! That is what makes them fascinating! The way their... capitalist accessibility masks their deeper revolutionary potential. Like Warhol, but with more... melody.

a-Across the room, PAUL MCCARTNEY and JANE ASHER walk past, looking at the art. Clive's eyes widen behind his glasses.

c-CLIVE
p-(lowering his voice conspiratorially)
d-In fact, I am developing what I call 'aesthetic penetration protocols' to understand how their influence might... reshape the cultural landscape.

c-ART CRITIC 2
p-(confused)
d-Aesthetic penetration protocols? That sounds like something else entirely.

c-CLIVE
d-No, no! It is... artistic methodology. Very sophisticated. I am trying to determine if their album covers might benefit from more... existential weight.

a-He pulls out another sketch. This one shows a mock album cover with each Beatles head replaced by a different sad-looking Victorian monument.

c-CLIVE
d-Think about it. The burden of fame. The weight of history. The fountain as metaphor for...

c-ART CRITIC 1
p-(interrupting, trying to be polite)
d-That's... interesting. But if you'll excuse us, we were just discussing the Francis Bacon retrospective.

a-They start backing away slowly. Clive, convinced he's making progress, follows them.

c-CLIVE
d-But the cultural insurgency! The semiotic overload! Have you considered that the Shirley Temple doll on the Sgt. Pepper's cover might actually be...

a-The critics practically run into another room. Clive stands there, sketchbook in hand, convinced of his intellectual superiority.

c-CLIVE
p-(to himself, adjusting beret)
d-They are intimidated by my insights. Typical bourgeois reaction to revolutionary thought.

s-INT. MI5 SURVEILLANCE VAN – CONTINUOUS

a-Nigel watches the critics flee from Clive. He takes another bite of his sandwich. The cheese is sweating slightly in the summer heat.

c-NIGEL
p-(to his sandwich)
d-Well, that's another potential international incident narrowly avoided. 

a-He checks his watch. Only four more hours of this. Only four more years of this, if his calculations are correct.

c-NIGEL
p-(sighs)
d-At least the sandwich has structural integrity.

a-He takes another bite. The bread falls apart in his hands. Of course it does.

s-EXT. CHELSEA GALLERY – LATER

a-Clive is now standing by himself, still in his "Klaus" persona. He's attempting to look contemplative while sketching. Two teenage girls walk past, whispering and pointing.

c-GIRL 1
d-Who's that?

c-GIRL 2
d-I think he's one of Paul's art friends. He looks... intense.

a-Clive overhears this. He straightens up, puffing his chest out slightly. He believes his cover is working perfectly.

a-From the surveillance van, Nigel watches through binoculars. He lowers them slowly.

c-NIGEL
p-(muttering)
d-We're spying on art critics. I'm a grown man eating a sandwich in a van while my partner pretends to be German to influence album cover design through bad drawings. 

a-He looks at his reflection in the rearview mirror.

c-NIGEL
d-What happened to my life?

t-FADE OUT.
