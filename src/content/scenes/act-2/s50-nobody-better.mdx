t-FADE IN:

s-INT. TWICKENHAM FILM STUDIOS - DAY (JANUARY 3, 1969)

a-The soundstage is vast and cold. Concrete floors, industrial rigging, fluorescent lights that hum at a frequency designed for warehouses, not music. Film cameras on their rigs stand at intervals around the room — not yet rolling, but present. Watching. There are more of them than feel comfortable.

a-The Beatles' equipment has been set up in the center of the space. It looks small here. The same amps and drums that filled the Cavern, that filled Shea Stadium, reduced to an island in an institutional sea.

a-PAUL MCCARTNEY (26) is alone. He's arrived early — or perhaps the others are simply late. He sits at the upright piano and plays.

a-Not a Beatles song. Not anything in progress. He plays Samuel Barber's Adagio for Strings — the American elegy, the music that plays at funerals, at the end of things. He doesn't know that's what it is. He's just playing something beautiful because the room is empty and he can.

a-The cameras are not rolling yet. No one sees this. It happens anyway.

a-A DOOR opens at the far end of the soundstage. GEORGE HARRISON (25) and RINGO STARR (28) come in together, coats still on, the cold of January still on them. Paul stops playing, looks up.

c-RINGO
p-(nodding at Paul's face)
d-The beard suits you.

c-PAUL
p-(laughing, uncertain)
d-Yeah, well. I don't know about it, really. I'm just sort of—

c-RINGO
d-Captain.

a-General laughter. The room is easier already.

c-GEORGE
d-You just keep on until you do know about it.

c-PAUL
d-Exactly, yeah.

a-George sits with his guitar. Ringo settles behind the drums. Paul stays at the piano. The three of them fall naturally into something — not a rehearsal, not a session, just the old reflex of being musicians in a room together. After a few bars of nothing in particular, someone finds the opening of The Coasters' 'Three Cool Cats' and suddenly they're all in it, grinning.

a-JOHN LENNON (28) arrives in the middle of this. He takes in the room — the cameras, the lights, his three friends playing a Coasters song for no reason — and something in his face relaxes. He hangs up his coat, sits at the organ, finds the key, joins in.

a-They play badly and happily. It's the first music of the new year.

a-Later. The Three Cool Cats have given way to something older. John and Paul are singing together, leaning into a song from the Quarrymen days — an early composition neither of them has played in years, unearthed the way old friends unearth old jokes.

c-JOHN
d-Won't you please say goodbye / This love has long since gone cold / Won't you please say goodbye / How many times must you be told...

a-Paul harmonizes without thinking, the way you harmonize with someone you've been singing with since you were teenagers, the way the voices fit together before you've decided to let them.

a-Then 'One After 909'. They fall into it, and Paul laughs halfway through, because he's just understood it for the first time.

c-PAUL
d-I mean, I never sort of knew what it was about before. She's on a train—

c-JOHN
d-Yeah.

c-PAUL
d-And he sort of—

c-JOHN
d-And he goes to the station, and misses it.

c-PAUL
p-(laughing)
d-But he goes back and finds that it was the wrong number. So—

c-GEORGE
d-"Wrong location."

c-JOHN
p-(officious voice)
d-To rhyme with station, you know.

c-PAUL
p-(still laughing)
d-That's great, that. "The rail man said, 'You've got the wrong location.'" Our kid's been saying "you should do that one" for years, you know. And we've always sort of — "Yeah, well, you know, Mike. You don't understand about these things."

a-The laughter dies down the way laughter does — not stopped, just spent. The room reasserts itself. The fluorescent lights hum. A CAMERA OPERATOR makes a small adjustment to his rig, a mechanical sound in the silence.

a-George has been thinking about something. He starts talking, the way he sometimes does, as if arriving at a conclusion he's been working toward all morning.

c-GEORGE
d-That band — Bonnie and Delaney. They're more together than we've ever been. As a band. Everyone who's been to see them playing — their lives change. It's really exciting when you hear a real groove.

c-JOHN
d-You see, I think when we've done a month of this, and we do the album — we should use what we've got, the togetherness. To do the album. And then maybe another one a month later. If we get into the swinging of playing.

c-PAUL
d-We should organise our career, man. Get our career together. This is the idea of this — to get us so we quite enjoy doing it again.

c-GEORGE
d-When we learn them all properly, we'd dig it. When we know every one, if somebody could say "that one," and we just play it. That'd be great.

a-He pauses. Picks at his guitar strings, not playing, just touching them.

c-GEORGE (CONT'D)
d-It's like hard work, really, to do it. And it's a drag to try and — I want to remember all the words, and really get 'em the way I want to get 'em. And get the guitar. When you're not ready for it. But that... that's all right.

c-PAUL
p-(receptive)
d-I can see it. So we're just working.

c-GEORGE
d-We've got to get through that bit of shit of the meeting until we get it together again. But — there's so much to get out, you know.

c-PAUL
d-Yeah, right.

a-George looks down at his guitar. When he speaks again it's quiet enough that John has to lean in slightly from the organ.

c-GEORGE (CONT'D)
p-(sincere)
d-And there's nobody better to get it out with than us. For me, anyway, really. In my heart of hearts.

a-Paul is quiet for a long moment.

c-PAUL
p-(reserved)
d-Yeah.

c-GEORGE (CONT'D)
d-It should be where — if you write a song, I feel as though I've wrote it. And mine, vice versa. The last album — that's the only album so far I've really tried to get involved with.

a-Nobody says anything. The statement hangs there — generous, a little sad, a little late.

a-Somewhere above them the fluorescent lights tick and hum. A camera turns on its mount, repositioning. The sound of the mechanism fills the silence.

a-Ringo picks up his sticks. He taps the hi-hat once, twice. An invitation more than a rhythm.

a-One by one the others pick up their instruments. They begin to play — slowly at first, then finding it. The Third Man theme, of all things, because someone's fingers found the notes without intending to. They don't talk. They just play, in this cold and institutional room, the way they always have.

a-The cameras watch. The fluorescent lights hum.

t-FADE OUT.
