t-FADE IN:

s-INT. BRIAN EPSTEIN'S OFFICE - NIGHT (AUGUST 1966)

a-A pristine, orderly office. Neat stacks of files, a gleaming mahogany desk, gold records on the wall. Everything under control.

a-Except it isn’t.

a-BRIAN EPSTEIN (31) sits behind his desk, tie loosened, sleeves rolled. He looks exhausted. Around him: open ledgers, telex machines humming, a half-empty glass of whiskey.

a-He rubs his temples, staring at a TELEGRAM from America:

a-TEXT: "STOP ALL BEATLES BROADCASTS IMMEDIATELY — MULTIPLE STATIONS — BIBLE BELT BACKLASH ESCALATING — URGENT GUIDANCE REQUIRED"

a-He picks up a thick file labeled "MERCHANDISING — NORTH AMERICA." He flips to a page with Seltab’s logo and a damning note in his own handwriting: "10% of profits. What was I thinking?"

a-He closes it. Opens another: a letter from George’s solicitor about "independent artistic direction." A third: a note from John’s accountant about "unusual art acquisitions."

a-Brian stands, walks to the window. Below, London glitters. He used to feel like he owned this city. Tonight he feels like it’s swallowing him.

c-BRIAN
p-(softly, to himself)
d-They were boys. Now they’re... businesses.

a-He returns to the desk, picks up the phone. Dials a number from memory. Waits.

c-BRIAN
p-(into phone, strained calm)
d-Yes, it’s Brian Epstein. I need to speak with someone about the American tour situation.
p-(beat)
d-No, not the promoter. The other thing. The... religious thing.

a-He listens, his face tightening.

c-BRIAN
d-I understand. But we can’t simply cancel. The contracts...
p-(beat)
d-Of course I understand the principle. I’m trying to prevent a riot.

a-He hangs up. Leans back, eyes closed. The phone RINGS again immediately. He lets it.

a-A KNOCK at the door. His assistant, PETER BROWN, peers in.

c-PETER BROWN
d-Brian? It’s past midnight. Should I—

c-BRIAN
d-One more call, Peter. Then home.

a-Peter nods, closes the door quietly. Brian stares at the ringing phone as if it’s an enemy combatant. Finally, he picks it up.

c-BRIAN
d-Epstein.

a-We don’t hear the other end. We only see Brian’s face shift from exhaustion to something worse: dawning recognition that this is not a crisis he can manage.

c-BRIAN
d-When?
p-(beat)
d-I see. And the boys... they don’t know yet?
p-(beat)
d-Right. I’ll tell them. Tomorrow.

a-He hangs up slowly. Stares at the wall of gold records. They look less like trophies and more like evidence.

c-BRIAN
p-(whispering)
d-What have I built?

a-He reaches for the whiskey glass. His hand trembles slightly.

a-The office is immaculate. The man inside is fraying.

t-FADE OUT.
