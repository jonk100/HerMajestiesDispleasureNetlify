t-FADE IN:

s-INT. KENWOOD - AFTERNOON (LATE 1968)

a-The house is quiet. Too quiet.

a-CYNTHIA LENNON (27) moves through the rooms with practiced efficiency. Dusting, straightening, maintaining the home that’s supposed to be their sanctuary.

a-She stops in John’s study. The room feels different lately. Crowded with things she doesn’t understand.

a-On his desk: the glued-together teacup. The instruction booklet. A small stack of letters with unfamiliar handwriting.

a-She picks up one of the letters. The paper is expensive, the handwriting precise. She doesn’t read it—she doesn’t have to. The weight of it is enough.

a-She places it back. Exactly where it was.

a-She moves to the bookshelf. Runs her finger over the spines: philosophy, religion, art theory. Books she’s never seen before.

a-She pulls one out: “The Tao of Physics.” Opens it to a marked page. John’s handwriting in the margin:

a-“Everything is connected. Except us.”

a-Cynthia closes the book. Puts it back. Her hands are steady.

a-She walks to the window. Looks out at the garden. Julian is outside with a nanny, laughing. Normal. Good.

a-But inside this house, nothing is normal anymore.

a-She goes to their bedroom. Opens John’s wardrobe. His clothes are there, but they feel like costumes. She finds a small box tucked in the back.

a-Inside: a single white feather. A dried flower. A photograph of Yoko she wasn’t meant to see.

a-She doesn’t react. No tears. No anger. Just a long, quiet breath.

a-She puts the box back. Closes the wardrobe door.

a-She stands in the middle of the room. The house around her feels like a museum of someone else’s life.

a-She walks to the phone. Picks it up. Dials a number from memory.

c-CYNTHIA
d-(into phone, calm)
d-Hello, Mimi? It’s Cynthia.
p-(beat)
d-No, everything’s fine. I was just wondering... would it be alright if Julian and I came to stay for a few days?
p-(beat)
d-Just a visit. It’s time for a visit.

a-She listens, nods.

c-CYNTHIA
d-Thank you. We’ll see you tomorrow.

a-She hangs up. Looks around the room one last time.

a-She’s not leaving because of anger. She’s leaving because the space beside John in their bed has been empty for months, and she’s the only one who didn’t want to admit it.

a-She walks out of the room, down the stairs, her steps quiet. Not like she’s fleeing. Like she’s closing a door she should have closed a long time ago.

t-FADE OUT.
