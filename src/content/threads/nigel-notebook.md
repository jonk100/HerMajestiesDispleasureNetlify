# Nigel's Notebook

A physical surveillance notebook. Small, soft leather cover, the kind that fits in a jacket pocket. Nigel has carried it since the beginning. The institution probably issued it. He writes in it. Most of what he writes is procedural — observations, notations, log entries. The kind of writing the institution requires.

But some of what he writes is the real record.

**The Episode 2 notation:** Nigel observes John explaining guitar string composition to his young son Julian. John says the instrument is capable of "stories about love." Nigel writes it down. Beneath: "No action required." This is his accurate assessment. It is also the series' moral center stated plainly, before the institution adds pressure to revise it.

**The 'yet' addition:** At some later point — different ink, different pen, different pressure — someone (Nigel, under institutional duress; the series doesn't specify) adds: "Yet." One word. The institutional revision of the original verdict. The implication that the decision will eventually be reversed.

**The crossing-out:** December 1980. The radio. John is dead. Nigel opens the drawer. He finds the page. He looks at 'yet.' He takes his pen. He crosses it out. Slowly. One line. He restores the original verdict. 'No action required.' The series' final moral act.

The notebook rhymes with Mountbatten's corridor notebook in Episode 6 — private moral registrations in an institution that doesn't have a mechanism for them. Both men write things down that no one will read. Mountbatten writes 'God help us.' Nigel crosses out 'yet.' One is gone by the time the other crosses the word out.
