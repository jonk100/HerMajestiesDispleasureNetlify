Nigel is performing routine surveillance when John's voice comes through the headphones, explaining guitar strings to his son Julian: 'It's got stories in it. And we don't want the stories to fall out.'

Nigel freezes. Pen stops tapping. The surveillance apparatus captures something it was never designed to receive — an unguarded private moment, John as father rather than target. The headphones are the wrong instrument for this particular transmission.

'Stories about love. And about being sad sometimes.' John's voice fades as he moves away from the microphone. Nigel can hear just enough to understand what he is and not enough to fully have it. The operation's moral logic is now complicated by a specific human detail that cannot be processed as evidence.
