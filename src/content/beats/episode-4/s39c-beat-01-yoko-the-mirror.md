The harmony on 'Get Back.' John glancing at Yoko before joining Paul's harmony. 'She doesn't say anything. She doesn't gesture. She just meets his eyes, gives a small, almost imperceptible nod. Grounding him.' Paul noticing. His expression tightening. The second glance — Paul cold: 'Does she need to approve everything you say?'

John: 'I'm just trying to find the right words, Paul.'

Paul: 'Are they your words or hers?'

The harmony is working. A brief window of old connection. Yoko's nod observed by Paul. The creative session becomes a territorial confrontation. The music stops being the subject.

'Paul's resentment becomes clear. It's not really about Yoko. It's about losing his creative partner, his other half. Yoko is just the mirror reflecting what Paul can't accept: John has found a different way to survive. John's distance isn't cruelty. It's survival. And Paul's anger isn't about Yoko. It's about displacement.'

The narrator's analysis ('not about Yoko, about displacement') is correctly placed here as the scene's thesis rather than its action. The question is whether the stage direction needs to deliver it explicitly or whether the audience has already arrived there. Consider cutting the last two paragraphs of narration and trusting the scene. 'Are they your words or hers?' followed by John not answering, just looking at Yoko, is a complete scene. The analysis can be cut.
